## Question 2

Under the EU AI Act, what are the mandatory verification steps a hospital (as a 'Deployer') must complete before putting a high-risk AI medical device into service?

### Answer

### **Direct Answer**
Under the **EU AI Act**, a hospital (as a **deployer**) of a **high-risk AI medical device** must complete the following **mandatory verification steps** before putting the system into service:

1. **Verify the Provider’s Compliance Documentation**
   - Confirm that the **provider** has drawn up the **EU declaration of conformity** (Article 47 EU AI Act) and that the device bears the **CE marking**.
   - Ensure the provider has compiled **technical documentation** (Annex IV EU AI Act) demonstrating compliance with the **requirements in Chapter III, Section 2** (e.g., risk management, data governance, transparency, human oversight, accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity).
   - Check that the provider has registered the high-risk AI system in the **EU database** (Article 49 EU AI Act) and that the hospital has access to the **registration number**.

2. **Assess the Intended Purpose and Compliance with MDR/IVDR (if applicable)**
   - Since the AI system is a **medical device**, the hospital must ensure it complies with **MDR 2017/745** (or **IVDR 2017/746** if in vitro diagnostic).
   - Verify that the device has undergone the **appropriate conformity assessment procedure** under **MDR/IVDR** (e.g., involvement of a **notified body** for Class IIa/IIb/III devices).
   - Confirm that the **AI Act requirements (Chapter III, Section 2)** have been integrated into the **MDR/IVDR conformity assessment** (Article 43(3) EU AI Act).

3. **Review the Provider’s Instructions and Information**
   - Obtain and review the **instructions for use (IFU)** and any **additional documentation** provided by the provider (Article 13 EU AI Act).
   - Ensure the IFU includes:
     - The **intended purpose** of the AI system.
     - **Performance characteristics** (e.g., accuracy, limitations).
     - **Risks** associated with use (e.g., false positives/negatives, bias).
     - **Human oversight measures** (e.g., how clinicians should intervene).
     - **Post-market monitoring (PMM) obligations** (e.g., reporting adverse events).

4. **Conduct a Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment (FRIA) (if applicable)**
   - If the AI system is used in a **high-risk context** (e.g., patient triage, diagnostic support, treatment recommendations), the hospital must perform a **Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment (FRIA)** (Article 27 EU AI Act).
   - The FRIA must evaluate:
     - Potential **impacts on fundamental rights** (e.g., non-discrimination, privacy, autonomy).
     - **Bias and fairness** in decision-making.
     - **Transparency and explainability** of the AI system’s outputs.
     - **Safeguards** to mitigate risks (e.g., human review, fallback mechanisms).

5. **Ensure Human Oversight is in Place**
   - Verify that the AI system is designed to allow **effective human oversight** (Article 14 EU AI Act).
   - Confirm that **qualified personnel** (e.g., clinicians) are trained to:
     - **Understand the AI system’s limitations**.
     - **Override or disregard** the AI’s outputs when necessary.
     - **Monitor performance** and report anomalies.

6. **Implement Post-Market Monitoring (PMM) and Reporting**
   - Establish a **post-market monitoring (PMM) system** to track the AI system’s performance in real-world use (Article 72 EU AI Act).
   - Ensure mechanisms are in place to:
     - **Collect and document** data on the AI system’s performance, errors, and adverse events.
     - **Report serious incidents** to the provider and **competent authorities** (e.g., via **Eudamed** for medical devices).
     - **Update the system** if the provider releases patches or improvements.

7. **Maintain Records of Compliance**
   - Keep records of:
     - The **EU declaration of conformity** and **CE marking**.
     - The **technical documentation** provided by the provider.
     - The **FRIA (if conducted)**.
     - **Training records** for staff using the AI system.
     - **PMM data** and any **corrective actions** taken.

## Question 3

How do the obligations for High-Risk AI systems under the AI Act overlap with existing MDR requirements, and which 'AI-specific' gaps should a hospital check that aren't covered by a standard CE mark?

### Answer

### **Direct Answer: Overlap and AI-Specific Gaps Between the AI Act and MDR**

The **EU AI Act** and **MDR 2017/745** overlap significantly for **high-risk AI systems that are also medical devices** (e.g., AI-driven diagnostic or therapeutic tools). The AI Act explicitly defers to the MDR’s conformity assessment procedures (Article 8(2) AI Act) but **adds AI-specific requirements** that go beyond the MDR’s scope. Below is a structured breakdown of the **overlaps** and **AI-specific gaps** that a hospital (as a deployer) should verify, even if the device already bears a CE mark under the MDR.

---

### **1. Overlapping Obligations (MDR and AI Act)**
These requirements are **largely aligned** between the two regulations, meaning compliance with the MDR typically satisfies the AI Act’s corresponding provisions.

| **Obligation**               | **MDR Reference**                          | **AI Act Reference**                     | **Notes**                                                                                     |
|------------------------------|-------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Risk Management**          | Annex I, Point 3 (MDR)                    | Article 9 (AI Act)                       | Both require continuous, iterative risk management throughout the lifecycle.                 |
| **Technical Documentation**  | Annex II, III (MDR)                       | Article 11 + Annex IV (AI Act)           | The AI Act’s technical documentation (Annex IV) **complements** the MDR’s requirements.       |
| **Quality Management System**| Article 10(9) + Annex IX (MDR)            | Article 17 (AI Act)                      | The AI Act explicitly allows integration of QMS processes with MDR requirements (Article 8(2)).|
| **Post-Market Surveillance** | Articles 83–86 + Annex III (MDR)          | Article 72 (AI Act)                      | Both require proactive monitoring, reporting, and corrective actions.                        |
| **Clinical Evaluation**      | Article 61 + Annex XIV (MDR)              | Article 10(2)(f) (AI Act)                | The AI Act does not replace MDR’s clinical evaluation but **adds AI-specific data governance**.|
| **Conformity Assessment**    | Article 52 + Annexes IX–XI (MDR)          | Article 43 (AI Act)                      | The AI Act **defers to MDR’s conformity assessment** for medical devices (Article 43(3)).     |
| **Registration in Database** | Article 29 (MDR, Eudamed)                 | Article 49 (AI Act, EU Database)         | The AI Act requires **additional registration** in its EU database (Article 71), even if the device is already registered in Eudamed. |

**Key Takeaway:**
For **high-risk AI medical devices**, the MDR’s conformity assessment (e.g., Annex IX) **covers most AI Act requirements**, but the AI Act **adds AI-specific layers** (e.g., data governance, transparency, and fundamental rights impact assessments).

---

### **2. AI-Specific Gaps Not Covered by MDR (Critical for Hospitals to Verify)**
The AI Act introduces **new obligations** that are **not addressed in the MDR** but are **mandatory for high-risk AI systems**, including medical devices. Hospitals (as deployers) must ensure these gaps are filled, even if the device is CE-marked under the MDR.

#### **A. Data and Data Governance (Article 10 AI Act)**
**Gap:**
The MDR focuses on **clinical data** and **risk management** but does **not** explicitly require:
- **Training/validation/testing data governance** (e.g., bias mitigation, representativeness, data quality).
- **Data provenance tracking** (e.g., origin of training data, consent for data use).
- **Data security measures** specific to AI training pipelines.

**What Hospitals Should Check:**
✅ **Data Quality:**
   - Is the training/validation/testing data **representative** of the target population?
   - Are there **biases** in the data that could lead to discriminatory outcomes (e.g., underrepresentation of certain ethnic groups in diagnostic AI)?
   - Is the data **labelled accurately** (e.g., for supervised learning models)?

✅ **Data Provenance:**
   - Can the provider **demonstrate the origin** of the training data (e.g., anonymized patient records, public datasets)?
   - Was **informed consent** obtained for data used in training (if applicable)?

✅ **Data Security:**
   - Are there **measures to prevent data poisoning** (e.g., adversarial attacks on training data)?
   - Is the **data storage and processing** compliant with GDPR (e.g., pseudonymization, encryption)?

**Regulatory Text:**
> *"High-risk AI systems which make use of techniques involving the training of AI models with data shall be developed on the basis of training, validation and testing data sets that meet the quality criteria referred to in paragraphs 2 to 5 whenever such data sets are used."* **[14] Article 10(1) AI Act**

---

#### **B. Transparency and Information to Deployers (Article 13 AI Act)**
**Gap:**
The MDR requires **instructions for use (IFU)** but does **not** mandate:
- **AI-specific transparency** (e.g., explaining model limitations, uncertainty metrics).
- **Human oversight instructions** (e.g., how clinicians should interpret AI outputs).
- **Performance metrics** (e.g., accuracy, robustness, cybersecurity resilience).

**What Hospitals Should Check:**
✅ **Explainability:**
   - Does the provider **explain how the AI system works** (e.g., decision logic, confidence scores)?
   - Are **uncertainty metrics** provided (e.g., "This prediction has a 70% confidence level")?

✅ **Human Oversight:**
   - Are there **clear instructions** on how clinicians should **override or question** AI outputs?
   - Is the **level of automation** (e.g., fully autonomous vs. decision-support) clearly stated?

✅ **Performance Metrics:**
   - Are **accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity** metrics disclosed (e.g., false positive/negative rates, adversarial attack resilience)?
   - Are **failure modes** documented (e.g., "The system may fail in cases of rare diseases")?

**Regulatory Text:**
> *"High-risk AI systems shall be designed and developed in such a way, including with appropriate human-machine interface tools, that they can be effectively overseen by natural persons during the period in which the AI system is in use."* **[14] Article 14(1) AI Act**

---

#### **C. Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment (Article 27 AI Act)**
**Gap:**
The MDR focuses on **safety and performance** but does **not** require an assessment of:
- **Impact on fundamental rights** (e.g., privacy, non-discrimination, autonomy).
- **Ethical risks** (e.g., over-reliance on AI, dehumanization of care).

**What Hospitals Should Check:**
✅ **Fundamental Rights Risks:**
   - Does the AI system **discriminate** against certain patient groups (e.g., based on age, gender, ethnicity)?
   - Does it **infringe on patient autonomy** (e.g., by making decisions without human review)?
   - Does it **violate privacy** (e.g., by processing sensitive health data without adequate safeguards)?

✅ **Ethical Considerations:**
   - Is there a **risk of over-reliance** on AI (e.g., clinicians blindly following AI recommendations)?
   - Does the AI system **dehumanize care** (e.g., replacing clinician-patient interaction with automated decisions)?

**Regulatory Text:**
> *"Deployers of a high-risk AI system that are public authorities, agencies or bodies, or natural or legal persons acting on their behalf, shall, prior to putting into service or using a high-risk AI system, perform an assessment of its impact on fundamental rights."* **[14] Article 27(1) AI Act**

---

#### **D. Automatically Generated Logs (Article 19 AI Act)**
**Gap:**
The MDR requires **traceability** (e.g., UDI, post-market surveillance) but does **not** mandate:
- **Automated logging of AI decisions** (e.g., for auditability, accountability).
- **Retention of logs** for **6 months** (minimum requirement under AI Act).

**What Hospitals Should Check:**
✅ **Logging Capabilities:**
   - Does the AI system **automatically log** all inputs, outputs, and decisions?
   - Are logs **tamper-proof** and **retained for at least 6 months**?
   - Can logs be **exported for audits** (e.g., by regulators or internal compliance teams)?

✅ **Auditability:**
   - Can the hospital **reconstruct** how an AI decision was made (e.g., for incident investigations)?
   - Are logs **compatible with GDPR** (e.g., anonymized where necessary)?

**Regulatory Text:**
> *"High-risk AI systems shall technically allow for the automatic recording of events (‘logs’) over the duration of the lifetime of the system. Those logs shall be capable of recording the events relevant for identifying national-level risks and substantial modifications to the system throughout its lifecycle."* **[14] Article 19(1) AI Act**

---

#### **E. Cybersecurity and Robustness (Article 15 AI Act)**
**Gap:**
The MDR requires **risk management** (Annex I) but does **not** explicitly address:
- **AI-specific cybersecurity risks** (e.g., adversarial attacks, model inversion).
- **Robustness against input manipulation** (e.g., "fooling" an AI model with perturbed data).

**What Hospitals Should Check:**
✅ **Adversarial Robustness:**
   - Is the AI system **resistant to adversarial attacks** (e.g., small perturbations in input data that cause incorrect outputs)?
   - Has the provider **tested for robustness** against common attack vectors (e.g., data poisoning, model evasion)?

✅ **Cybersecurity Measures:**
   - Are there **measures to prevent unauthorized access** to the AI model (e.g., API security, encryption)?
   - Is the AI system **protected against model inversion attacks** (e.g., reconstructing training data from outputs)?

**Regulatory Text:**
> *"High-risk AI systems shall be designed and developed in such a way that they achieve an appropriate level of accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity, and that they perform consistently in those respects throughout their lifecycle."* **[14] Article 15(1) AI Act**

### **3. Summary: AI-Specific Gaps vs. MDR Coverage**
| **Requirement**               | **Covered by MDR?** | **AI Act Gap?** | **What Hospitals Must Verify**                                                                 |
|-------------------------------|---------------------|-----------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Risk Management**           | ✅ (Annex I)        | ❌              | MDR covers general risk management; AI Act adds **AI-specific risk scenarios**.               |
| **Clinical Evaluation**       | ✅ (Article 61)     | ⚠️              | MDR covers clinical data; AI Act adds **data governance for training data**.                  |
| **Technical Documentation**   | ✅ (Annex II, III)  | ⚠️              | MDR covers device specs; AI Act adds **AI model details (Annex IV)**.                         |
| **Post-Market Surveillance**  | ✅ (Articles 83–86) | ❌              | MDR covers PMS; AI Act adds **AI-specific monitoring (e.g., model drift)**.                   |
| **Data Governance**           | ❌                  | ✅ (Article 10) | **New requirement**: Training data quality, bias mitigation, provenance tracking.             |
| **Transparency**              | ⚠️ (IFU only)       | ✅ (Article 13) | **New requirement**: Explainability, human oversight instructions, performance metrics.      |
| **Fundamental Rights Impact** | ❌                  | ✅ (Article 27) | **New requirement**: Assessment of discrimination, privacy, autonomy risks.                  |
| **Automated Logging**         | ❌                  | ✅ (Article 19) | **New requirement**: Tamper-proof logs of AI decisions for 6+ months.                         |
| **Cybersecurity**             | ⚠️ (General)        | ✅ (Article 15) | **New requirement**: AI-specific robustness (adversarial attacks, model inversion).           |

### **4. Practical Steps for Hospitals**
To ensure compliance with **both MDR and AI Act**, hospitals should:
1. **Request AI-Specific Documentation from Providers:**
   - **Data governance report** (Article 10 AI Act).
   - **Fundamental rights impact assessment** (Article 27 AI Act).
   - **Cybersecurity and robustness testing results** (Article 15 AI Act).
   - **Automated logging capabilities** (Article 19 AI Act).

2. **Conduct Internal Audits:**
   - Verify that **training data is representative and unbiased**.
   - Ensure **human oversight mechanisms** are in place (e.g., clinician review of AI outputs).
   - Test **adversarial robustness** (e.g., can the AI be fooled by manipulated inputs?).

3. **Update Contracts and Procurement Policies:**
   - Include **AI Act compliance clauses** in contracts with providers.
   - Require **ongoing transparency** (e.g., updates on model performance, data drift).

4. **Train Staff:**
   - Educate clinicians on **AI limitations** (e.g., when to trust vs. override AI outputs).
   - Train IT teams on **AI-specific cybersecurity risks**.

5. **Monitor Regulatory Updates:**
   - The **AI Office** and **MDCG** will publish **guidance** on AI-MDR interactions (expected by 2026).
   - Stay informed on **harmonized standards** for AI in medical devices.

### **5. Key Regulatory References**
1. **AI Act Deference to MDR:**
   > *"Where a product contains an AI system, to which the requirements of this Regulation as well as requirements of the Union harmonisation legislation listed in Section A of Annex I apply, providers shall be responsible for ensuring that their product is fully compliant with all applicable requirements under applicable Union harmonisation legislation."* **[3] Article 8(2) AI Act**

2. **MDR’s Risk Management vs. AI Act’s AI-Specific Risks:**
   > *"Risk management shall be understood as a continuous iterative process throughout the entire lifecycle of a device, requiring regular systematic updating."* **[15] Annex I, Point 3 MDR**
   > *"The risk management system shall comprise the following steps: [...] the evaluation of other risks possibly arising, based on the analysis of data and feedback from the AI system’s use in real-world settings."* **[14] Article 9(2)(c) AI Act**

3. **AI Act’s Data Governance Requirement (Not in MDR):**
   > *"Training, validation and testing data sets shall be subject to data governance and management practices appropriate for the intended purpose of the high-risk AI system."* **[14] Article 10(2) AI Act**

4. **Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment (New for AI):**
   > *"Deployers [...] shall, prior to putting into service or using a high-risk AI system, perform an assessment of its impact on fundamental rights."* **[14] Article 27(1) AI Act**

### **6. Conclusion**
- **Overlap:** The MDR’s conformity assessment (e.g., Annex IX) **covers most AI Act requirements** for high-risk AI medical devices.
- **AI-Specific Gaps:** The AI Act introduces **new obligations** (data governance, transparency, fundamental rights impact, logging, cybersecurity) that **go beyond the MDR**.
- **Hospital’s Role:** As a **deployer**, the hospital must **verify these gaps are addressed** by the provider, even if the device is CE-marked under the MDR. This includes **requesting AI-specific documentation, conducting audits, and training staff**.

**Final Recommendation:**
Hospitals should **treat AI Act compliance as an extension of MDR compliance** and **proactively engage with providers** to ensure all AI-specific requirements are met. Failure to do so could result in **non-compliance with the AI Act**, even if the device is MDR-certified.

## Question 4

When does a hospital transition from being a 'Deployer' to a 'Provider' under the EU AI Act if they modify a third-party AI tool or integrate it into their own clinical workflows?

### Answer

### **Direct Answer**
Under the **EU AI Act**, a hospital (as a **deployer**) transitions to being a **provider** — and thus assumes all **provider obligations** under **Article 16** — in **three specific circumstances** set out in **Article 25(1)**. These are:

1. **Re-branding**: The hospital puts its **name or trademark** on the high-risk AI system already placed on the market or put into service, **without contractual arrangements allocating provider obligations otherwise**;
2. **Substantial modification**: The hospital makes a **substantial modification** to a high-risk AI system that has already been placed on the market or put into service, **in such a way that it remains a high-risk AI system** pursuant to **Article 6**;
3. **Intended purpose change**: The hospital **modifies the intended purpose** of an AI system (including a general-purpose AI system) that was **not classified as high-risk**, in such a way that the system **becomes a high-risk AI system** pursuant to **Article 6**.

If **any** of these three conditions are met, the hospital is **deemed to be the provider** of the modified AI system and must comply with **all provider obligations** under **Article 16**, including conformity assessment, technical documentation, quality management, and CE marking.

---

### **Regulatory Context and Verbatim Quotes**

#### **1. Legal Definitions**
- **Deployer** (Article 3):
  > ‘deployer’ means a natural or legal person, public authority, agency or other body **using an AI system under its authority** except where the AI system is used in the course of a personal non-professional activity; **[1] Article 3**

- **Provider** (Article 3):
  > ‘provider’ means a natural or legal person, public authority, agency or other body that **develops an AI system** or a general-purpose AI model **or that has an AI system or a general-purpose AI model developed and places it on the market or puts the AI system into service under its own name or trademark**, whether for payment or free of charge; **[1] Article 3**

#### **2. Transition Triggers (Article 25(1))**
The **core provision** governing when a deployer becomes a provider is **Article 25(1)**:

> 1. Any distributor, importer, **deployer** or other third-party shall be considered to be a **provider** of a high-risk AI system for the purposes of this Regulation and shall be subject to the obligations of the provider under **Article 16**, in any of the following circumstances:
> (a) they put their **name or trademark** on a high-risk AI system already placed on the market or put into service, **without prejudice to contractual arrangements stipulating that the obligations are otherwise allocated**;
> (b) they make a **substantial modification** to a high-risk AI system that has already been placed on the market or has already been put into service in such a way that it **remains a high-risk AI system pursuant to Article 6**;
> (c) they **modify the intended purpose** of an AI system, including a general-purpose AI system, which has not been classified as high-risk and has already been placed on the market or put into service in such a way that the AI system concerned **becomes a high-risk AI system in accordance with Article 6**. **[1] Article 25(1)**

#### **3. Provider Obligations (Article 16)**
Once deemed a provider, the hospital must comply with **all obligations** under **Article 16**, including:

> Providers of high-risk AI systems shall:
> (a) ensure that their high-risk AI systems are compliant with the requirements set out in Section 2;
> (b) indicate on the high-risk AI system or, where that is not possible, on its packaging or its accompanying documentation, as applicable, their name, registered trade name or registered trade mark, the address at which they can be contacted;
> (c) have a quality management system in place which complies with Article 17;
> (d) keep the documentation referred to in Article 18;
> (e) when under their control, keep the logs automatically generated by their high-risk AI systems as referred to in Article 19;
> (f) ensure that the high-risk AI system undergoes the relevant conformity assessment procedure as referred to in Article 43, prior to its being placed on the market or put into service;
> (g) draw up an EU declaration of conformity in accordance with Article 47;
> (h) affix the CE marking to the high-risk AI system or, where that is not possible, on its packaging or its accompanying documentation, to indicate conformity with this Regulation, in accordance with Article 48;
> (i) comply with the registration obligations referred to in Article 49(1);
> (j) take the necessary corrective actions and provide information as required in Article 20;
> (k) upon a reasoned request of a national competent authority, demonstrate the conformity of the high-risk AI system with the requirements set out in Section 2;
> (l) ensure that the high-risk AI system complies with accessibility requirements in accordance with Directives (EU) 2016/2102 and (EU) 2019/882. **[3] Article 16**

---

### **Application to Hospital Scenarios**

#### **Scenario 1: Re-branding (Article 25(1)(a))**
- **Trigger**: The hospital **puts its name or trademark** on the AI system (e.g., relabels it as "Hospital X AI Diagnostic Tool").
- **Effect**: The hospital becomes the **provider** and assumes **all Article 16 obligations**.
- **Exception**: If **contractual arrangements** explicitly allocate provider obligations to the original developer, the hospital **does not** become the provider.

> (a) they put their name or trademark on a high-risk AI system already placed on the market or put into service, **without prejudice to contractual arrangements stipulating that the obligations are otherwise allocated**; **[1] Article 25(1)(a)**

#### **Scenario 2: Substantial Modification (Article 25(1)(b))**
- **Trigger**: The hospital makes a **substantial modification** to a **high-risk AI system** (e.g., retraining the model with hospital-specific data, altering its decision logic, or integrating it into a new clinical workflow that affects safety or performance).
- **Effect**: The hospital becomes the **provider** if the modification is **substantial** and the system **remains high-risk**.
- **Key Consideration**: The **AI Act does not define "substantial modification"**. However, **Recital 84** and **Article 25(1)(b)** indicate that the modification must be such that the system **remains a high-risk AI system pursuant to Article 6**.

> (b) they make a **substantial modification** to a high-risk AI system that has already been placed on the market or has already been put into service in such a way that it **remains a high-risk AI system pursuant to Article 6**; **[1] Article 25(1)(b)**

> (84) ... This would also be the case if that party makes a **substantial modification** to a high-risk AI system that has already been placed on the market or has already been put into service in a way that it **remains a high-risk AI system** in accordance with this Regulation... **[2] Recital 84**

#### **Scenario 3: Intended Purpose Change (Article 25(1)(c))**
- **Trigger**: The hospital **changes the intended purpose** of an AI system that was **not originally classified as high-risk**, in a way that **brings it within the scope of Article 6** (e.g., repurposing a general-purpose AI chatbot to diagnose medical conditions).
- **Effect**: The hospital becomes the **provider** of a **new high-risk AI system** and must comply with **all Article 16 obligations**.

> (c) they **modify the intended purpose** of an AI system, including a general-purpose AI system, which has not been classified as high-risk and has already been placed on the market or put into service in such a way that the AI system concerned **becomes a high-risk AI system in accordance with Article 6**. **[1] Article 25(1)(c)**

---

### **Balanced Analysis: When Does a Modification or Integration Become "Substantial"?**

The **AI Act does not provide a definition or exhaustive list** of what constitutes a **"substantial modification"**. This assessment must be made **case-by-case**, based on the **impact on safety, performance, and compliance with high-risk requirements**.

#### **Arguments FOR a Modification Being Substantial**
- **Retraining with new data**: If the hospital retrains the AI model with **hospital-specific data** (e.g., patient demographics, local clinical practices), this could alter the model’s **accuracy, robustness, or risk profile**, potentially requiring a **new conformity assessment**.
- **Integration into clinical workflows**: If the AI system is **embedded into a new clinical decision support system** that affects **diagnosis, treatment, or patient safety**, this may constitute a **substantial modification** because it changes the **risk profile** and **intended use**.
- **Alteration of decision logic**: If the hospital **modifies the decision thresholds, output interpretation, or post-processing rules**, this could affect the **safety and performance** of the system, triggering provider obligations.

> The risk management system shall be understood as a **continuous iterative process** planned and run throughout the entire lifecycle of a high-risk AI system, requiring **regular systematic review and updating**. **[12] Article 9(2)**

#### **Arguments AGAINST a Modification Being Substantial**
- **Minor updates**: If the hospital applies **routine software updates, bug fixes, or minor parameter adjustments** that do **not affect safety or performance**, this is **unlikely** to be considered substantial.
- **User interface changes**: If the hospital only **customises the user interface** (e.g., language, display format) without altering the **core AI model or decision logic**, this is **unlikely** to trigger provider obligations.
- **Pre-defined integrations**: If the AI system is **designed for interoperability** and the hospital integrates it into an **approved clinical workflow** without modifying the AI’s **core functionality**, this may **not** be considered substantial.

> The implementation of the aspects referred to in paragraph 1 shall be **proportionate to the size of the provider’s organisation**. Providers shall, in any event, respect the degree of rigour and the level of protection required to ensure the compliance of their high-risk AI systems with this Regulation. **[5] Article 17(2)**

---

### **Key Takeaways for Hospitals**
1. **Re-branding = Provider**: If the hospital puts its **name or trademark** on the AI system, it becomes the **provider** unless **contractual arrangements** state otherwise.
2. **Substantial Modification = Provider**: If the hospital **retrains, alters decision logic, or integrates the AI into a new clinical workflow** in a way that affects **safety or performance**, it is **likely** to be considered a **substantial modification**, triggering provider obligations.
3. **Intended Purpose Change = Provider**: If the hospital **repurposes a non-high-risk AI system** (e.g., a chatbot) for a **high-risk use case** (e.g., diagnosis), it becomes the **provider** of a **new high-risk AI system**.
4. **Case-by-Case Assessment**: The **AI Act does not provide a bright-line rule** for "substantial modification". Hospitals must **document their assessment** and, if in doubt, **consult notified bodies or competent authorities**.
5. **Documentation Obligation**: If a hospital **modifies an AI system** and concludes it is **not high-risk**, it must **document this assessment** and register it under **Article 49(2)**.

> A provider who considers that an AI system referred to in Annex III is not high-risk shall **document its assessment** before that system is placed on the market or put into service. Such provider shall be subject to the registration obligation set out in **Article 49(2)**. **[7] Article 6(4)**
